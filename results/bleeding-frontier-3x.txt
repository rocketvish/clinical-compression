A 55-year-old Caucasian male presented to the emergency department with a one-day history of intractable nausea and vomiting and was found in atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response. History: paroxysmal atrial fibrillation (had previously refused warfarin), congestive heart failure with preserved ejection fraction, coronary artery disease treated with a bare metal stent 6 months prior, diabetes mellitus type 2, hypertension, gout, and chronic kidney disease stage 3 (creatinine clearance 30 mL/min). Home medications: prasugrel, aspirin, atorvastatin, clonidine, furosemide, potassium chloride, amlodipine, insulin, allopurinol, and hydralazine.

Nausea and vomiting were managed with supportive care; he was started on heparin and diltiazem drips for atrial fibrillation. Cardiology recommended rivaroxaban for thromboembolism prophylaxis because the patient's primary objection to warfarin was the need for frequent monitoring and dose adjustment—producing triple therapy (rivaroxaban, prasugrel, aspirin).

Within 72 hours of initiating renally dosed rivaroxaban, recurrent nasal bleeding developed: five mild daytime episodes stopped with direct pressure and simple packing, but that night severe posterior nasal hemorrhage required posterior nasal packing. Aspirin was discontinued. When the packing was removed 5 days later, bleeding recurred. Cardiology discontinued rivaroxaban and switched prasugrel to clopidogrel. After 24 hours without rivaroxaban and no further hemorrhage, he was discharged.

Discussion: Aspirin–clopidogrel dual therapy is standard to prevent stent thrombosis but inferior to oral anticoagulants for stroke prevention in high-risk atrial fibrillation; oral anticoagulants reduce AF stroke risk but are inadequate as monotherapy after stenting. About 5–7% of PCI patients have AF or another chronic anticoagulation indication. The best-studied triple therapy—aspirin, warfarin, clopidogrel—showed greater reduction in cardiovascular events and mortality than dual antiplatelet therapy, but significantly more major bleeding in the first 6 months (OR 2.12; 95% CI 1.05–4.29; P=0.04). Bleeding with triple therapy runs ~3.7× higher than warfarin alone and ~4× aspirin monotherapy; adding warfarin to dual antiplatelet therapy raises 30-day relative bleeding risk to 2.2, with yearly bleeding incidence 12% versus 3.7% for aspirin plus clopidogrel. Warfarin plus clopidogrel alone reduces bleeding without increasing thrombotic events versus triple therapy.

Multiple factors raised this patient's bleeding risk: prasugrel produces greater platelet inhibition and more bleeding than clopidogrel, and advanced chronic kidney disease increases bleeding risk. In ROCKET AF (rivaroxaban vs warfarin in nonvalvular AF), patients with creatinine clearance <30 mL/min were excluded and those with clearance 30–49 mL/min received reduced rivaroxaban 15 mg daily. No published data describe kidney failure's effect on rivaroxaban triple-therapy bleeding risk.

When stroke risk outweighs bleeding risk and triple therapy is mandatory, strategies to reduce bleeding include using the lowest effective aspirin dose, adding a proton pump inhibitor for gastrointestinal prophylaxis, and using bare metal stents to minimize triple-therapy duration (based on insufficient evidence). This is believed to be the first reported case of bleeding from combined rivaroxaban, prasugrel, and aspirin. No monitoring methods exist for newer oral anticoagulants, warranting caution especially with comorbidities like chronic kidney disease.
