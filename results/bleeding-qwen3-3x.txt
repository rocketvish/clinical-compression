A 55-year-old Caucasian male presented to the emergency department with a one-day history of intractable nausea and vomiting. He was found to be in atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response. He had a known history of paroxysmal atrial fibrillation but had previously refused warfarin therapy. His past medical history included congestive heart failure with preserved ejection fraction, coronary artery disease treated with a bare metal stent six months prior, diabetes mellitus type 2, hypertension, gout, and chronic kidney disease stage 3 (creatinine clearance 30 mL/min). His home medications included prasugrel 10 mg daily, aspirin 81 mg daily, atorvastatin, clonidine, furosemide, potassium chloride, amlodipine, insulin, allopurinol, and hydralazine.

His nausea and vomiting were managed with supportive care, and he was started on heparin and diltiazem drip for his atrial fibrillation. Cardiology recommended rivaroxaban for thromboembolism prophylaxis due to the patient's objection to warfarin, which required frequent monitoring and dose adjustment. Rivaroxaban was initiated at a renal dose of 15 mg daily. Within 72 hours of starting rivaroxaban, the patient experienced recurrent nasal bleeding. He had five episodes of mild bleeding during the day, which stopped with direct pressure and simple packing. However, that night he experienced severe posterior nasal hemorrhage requiring posterior nasal packing. Aspirin was discontinued. When the packing was removed five days later, bleeding recurred. Cardiology reevaluated the antithrombotic regimen, discontinuing rivaroxaban and switching prasugrel to clopidogrel. After 24 hours without oral rivaroxaban or recurrence of hemorrhage, the patient was deemed stable for discharge.

This case highlights the risks of triple antithrombotic therapy, which includes aspirin, prasugrel, and rivaroxaban. The combination of these agents increases the risk of bleeding, as evidenced by the patient's severe nasal hemorrhage. Prasugrel, which provides greater platelet inhibition than clopidogrel, contributes to increased bleeding risk. The patient's chronic kidney disease stage 3 (creatinine clearance 30 mL/min) further increases bleeding risk, as rivaroxaban is renally dosed and patients with creatinine clearance <30 mL/min are excluded from clinical trials. There is no published data on the effect of kidney failure on bleeding risk with triple therapy involving rivaroxaban.

The case underscores the importance of balancing the risk of thrombotic events with the risk of bleeding when selecting antithrombotic regimens. In patients with comorbidities that increase bleeding risk, such as chronic kidney disease, careful consideration of antithrombotic therapy is essential. Strategies to reduce bleeding risk, such as using lower effective doses of aspirin and adding proton pump inhibitors, may be considered. However, the use of bare metal stents to minimize the duration of triple therapy remains based on insufficient evidence. This case represents the first reported bleeding complication associated with the combination of rivaroxaban, prasugrel, and aspirin.
